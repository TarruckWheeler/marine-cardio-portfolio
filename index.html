Environmental Health Meets Cardiology

How my marine research directly informs my future career in cardiovascular medicine

Air Pollution & Heart Disease

Coastal pollution from degraded ecosystems releases particulates and toxins that directly contribute to cardiovascular disease, hypertension, and stroke risk in waterfront communities.

Heavy Metal Exposure

My research on sediment contamination reveals high concentrations of copper and lead that bioaccumulate in seafood, contributing to oxidative stress and atherosclerosis.

Inflammatory Pathways

Environmental toxins from eutrophic systems trigger chronic inflammation, a key factor in cardiovascular disease progression that preventive cardiology must address.

Preventive Medicine

Understanding environmental health allows cardiologists to develop comprehensive prevention strategies that address root causes, not just symptoms of heart disease.

Community Health

Coastal communities face disproportionate cardiovascular risks from environmental degradation, requiring specialized care approaches and advocacy for environmental justice.

Ecosystem Restoration

Healthy coastal ecosystems provide protective health benefits through improved air quality, reduced toxin exposure, and enhanced community wellness opportunities.
